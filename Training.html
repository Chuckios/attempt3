Rock Climber's Training Manual

Goal setting

You're reading this because you want to improve. This obvious, generic desire should not be your goal. Your goal should be "I want to climb 10 5.12s in my gym by the end of the year" or "I want to send a super cool 5.11 outdoor route at my local crag". Be specific.

Mental Attitude

I wanted to psych myself up for the climbing gym one day so I watched a navy seal training video because those guys try pretty hard. From the video, this statement resonated with me: when you think you’ve given 100%, you’re only at 40% effort. For the next 3 months, you’re going to try hard and give 100% effort both in and outside the gym. If you leave exhausted, great! You’re improving. Outside the gym, I was drinking 5 liters of water a day to stay hydrated, sleeping consistently on time, and eating enough/well.

Training is stressful on your body. If you’re feeling not recovered enough or in pain, take an extra rest day. It’s better to rest more so you can give 100% effort than to go in and give 80% effort. 80 is not good enough.

ARC

ARC is stands Aerobic, Respiration, and Capillarity. The result of ARC training is you can climb for longer and recover faster. ARC training consists of climbing up and down for a set amount of time. I started at 15 minutes and added 5 minutes per week. Rest for 10 minutes between each set. Progression takes place by increasing the time on the wall and/or increasing the route difficulty. Purpose

Imagine this: there’s a super rad 5.11, 100 feet tall at your local crag. You’ve tried it a few times but you can only get halfway before you rest from the pump. This awesome route starts with 40 feet of juggy 5.10, 20 feet of crux, and finishes with 40 feet of juggy 5.10. Most of this route is 5.10 and jugs. You can climb 5.10. You can climb jugs! What’s going on?

A lot of outdoor routes have "filler" climbing. It’s like the crackers on the outside of an Oreo. You have to get through the outside to get into the creamy inside (the crux). In this scenario, the filler is the 5.10 before and after the crux. Prior to ARC training, 5.10 slowly saps your energy because you’re used to climbing only one or two in the gym before you rest.

After ARC training, you are a 5.10 superhero! Able to climb 5.10 for 30 minutes in a row without feeling tired or resting. Now the 40 feet of 5.10 at the beginning is your warm up! You breeze through it and get a quick shake before launching through the crux. It’s pumpy but make it through. The tough part is over! Now you have 40 more feet of 5.10, which you can climb easily without getting more tired. You may even recover some energy on the 5.10 since you’re an ARC superstar. As soon as you finish the last crux move, you know the route is in the bag. You clip the chains, lower, and high five your friends! This is what the product of ARC training looks like. Additionally, the sheer volume of climbing during ARC training hones your technique for efficient movement, clipping, and resting.

Your first ARC sessions

I was consistently climbing 5.12- in the gym at the time of my first ARC cycle. I struggled through 5.8 on a vertical wall for 15 minutes. Like any new exercise or activity, avoid discouragement and you will progress quickly as your body adapts. Going from climbing one route to up climbing and down climbing 30 routes in a row is a big change. You’ll discover the “sweet spot” for ARCing, which is climbing at a level that you can maintain for an extended amount of time that is moderately challenging. Mama bear style, not too easy, not too hard. You should not be barely making the moves and falling off. If you fall, get back on the wall immediately without rest, climb something easier, and keep going. It’s better to start a low grade and work your way up based on how you feel. Embrace the runner’s high.

If you climb too hard, you'll get massively pumped and won't be able to finish the exercise. If you climb too easily, you won’t be engaged or improve. Aim for 60-70% effort throughout the exercise. You can also adjust the difficulty within a set. If you feel yourself getting tired going up the 5.10 for the 10th time, do a few laps on a 5.7 to recover and re-evaluate. Avoid no hand rests because you recover too much on those and that defeats the purpose of keeping your arms loaded for an extended period.

ARCing is not mindless climbing. Take this opportunity to put 110% focus into climbing with good technique regardless of the grade or how long you’ve been climbing. Good technique is satisfying as you develop the ability to control your body and harness efficient movement up the wall. This will help maintain good technique on the wall while pumped on your project. This also improves your “mental endurance” for long single pitch routes or big multipitch days.

Progression

  • Increase the time on the wall. See the training table.
  • Increase the route difficulty.
  • Increase the steepness of the wall.
  • Do it on lead instead of TR.

As you move through the RCTM program, ARCing is your foundation. You will do a 15 – 20 min set to warm up or warm down after a workout. Use this time to stretch on the wall and prep your mind and body for training.

Bonus exercises

  • Run technique drills (silent feet, no re-adjusting on holds, etc).
  • If your gym has draws on the wall, bring a 10 foot rope, tie in, and clip the draws while you climb up and down. You'll improve at clipping while pumped and finding optimal body positions to clip from.
  • Add weight, using a weight vest or a camelback filled with water. This will help you acclimate to the rope drag or the weight of 20 quickdraws.

Hangboard

It’s time to increase finger strength! If you are unable to consistently climb 5.12- or V4, do not hangboard. Your tendons are not ready for the strain placed on them during hangboard training and you’ll have a high chance of injury.

Warm up

  1. ARC for 15 – 20 minutes.
  2. Warm up boulder ladder: climb 2 or 3 of boulders each grade up to your flash level. 20 minutes
  3. Limit bouldering: 2 or 3 attempts on boulders at your limit. 20 minutes
  4. Rest 10 minutes then it’s HB time!

Here are the HB excercises:

Repeaters:

Start with this one your first cycle. Hang for 10 seconds, rest for 5, repeat 6 times for a total of 90 seconds.

Rest 4 minutes. Do 8 sets of these.

RI warm up with one set on big jugs, 2 sets on big crimps, and then making it harder from there. For your first cycle, it’s okay to be conservative and only use the biggest crimps. You will still improve!

Max Hangs:

Attach some weight to your harness, hang on a medium to large edge (15mm-20mm) for 10 seconds. Rest 3 minutes. I would not recommend this exercise your first RCTM cycle.

Progression

For repeaters, you progress by hanging on smaller edges or adding a little weight on the same edge.

For max hangs, you progress by adding weight.

Campusboard

It’s time to increase power! If you are unable to consistently climb 5.12- or V4, do not campus board Your tendons are not ready for the strain placed on them during campus board training and you’ll have a high chance of injury.

Warm up

  1. ARC for 15 – 20 minutes.
  2. Warm up boulder ladder: climb 2 or 3 of boulders each grade up to your flash level. 20 minutes
  3. Limit bouldering: 2 or 3 attempts on boulders at your limit. 20 minutes
  4. Rest 10 minutes then it’s CB time!

Matching ladders

Start by matching on the lowest rung. Right hand goes up, left hand matches, repeat. Take a 2 minute break. Switch hands. 8 sets total.

Hand over hand

Start by matching on the lowest run. Right hand goes up. Left hand goes up without matching to the next run, repeat. 2 minute break, switch hands, 8 sets total.

Max ladders

Start by matching on the lowest run. Right hand goes up, skipping a run or two. Left hand goes up, passing the right hand and skipping a run or two. Repeat. 2 minute break, 8 sets total. I would not recommend this exercise for your first RCTM.

Progression

Start with matching ladders, then move to hand over hand, then max ladders. Make the edge smaller to make it harder. Skip more rungs on max ladders.

Power Endurance

PE training smashes everything together, including you as climb the bouldery 5.12 for the 4th time. Followed by a pumpy 5.11+. Then a crimpy 5.11. One more – a vertical 5.11-. Burning forearms included, free of charge. All within 30 minutes.

There are two benefits for PE training.

1: Physical: you can complete extended, tough sequences, fighting through huge amounts of pump and coming out on top.

2: Mental: you go for the next hold regardless of how you feel, there is no giving up before trying. Sometimes you’ll launch up and hit the next hold, surprising yourself!

Remember that you will be just as pumped using good technique versus flailing and throwing. Maintain precise, purposeful movement to efficiently get you up these difficult routes

Here are the PE exercises

4x4

Pick 4 boulder problems. Climb one, drop, run to the next one, and repeat until you’ve climbed 4 routes.

Rest for 4 minutes. Do 4 sets for a total of 16 boulders. Rest 10 minutes. Do it again. Play with the difficulty and order of routes. The goal is to be so pumped by the 16th route that you fall off the last move.

You can also do this on top rope or auto belay. I prefer doing this on the auto belay at my gym because I’m primarily a sport climber and like longer routes.

Linked Boulder Circuits

Pick 5 boulder problems near each. Pick for of them and climb up one, down another, up another, and down the last one. Rest 4 minutes between each one. Repeat 4 times. Rest 10 minutes. Do it again. The goal is to be so pumped by the 16th route that you fall off the last move.

Intervals

Find a route slightly below your onsight level. Time yourself climbing it. Rest for 2x the time it took you to climb the route. If you took 30 seconds to climb it, rest for 60 seconds. Repeating the climb/rest cycle 6 times. Rest 10 minutes, do it again.

Progression

Increase route difficulty. The goal is not to climb faster because you’re not loading your arms for an extended duration. I mostly did 4x4s because I don’t like downclimbing hard boulder problems. To make up for the rest I got while lowering on the auto belay, I picked harder routes.

Rest Days

ARC: one rest day per session. Two if you need to recover skin.

HB: two rest days.

CB: two rest days.

PE: two rest days.

Perf: one or two, depending on how you feel.

Performance

You’ve leveled up! It’s time to send that 5.11 at your local crag. You have great endurance, increased finger strength, heightened power, and pump-proof power endurance! Go try hard, clip the chains, and take a well-deserved victory whip. The performance phase is 3 weeks. Try to end the performance phase on a high note!

Rest Week

The last phase of RCTM is the hardest because you can’t climb for a week! Congrats on completing your first RCTM cycle, hopefully progressing, projecting, and succeeding. Give your body time to fully recover. Go hiking or something. Keep doing core workouts during this time. After the week, you can climb for fun or start a new cycle. Every time I do a RCTM cycle I move up about half a grade. Seems insignificant but in reality, you can move from 5.12+ to 5.13- in 6 months of training. This may have taken years with the “just climb” approach.
